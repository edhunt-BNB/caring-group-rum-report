The Richard Caring hospitality empire — Caprice Holdings Ltd, Troia (UK) Restaurants Ltd (The Ivy Collection) and 46 Berkeley Square Ltd (the Birley Clubs) — was acquired on 13 April 2026 by Abu Dhabi investment vehicle DIAFA, affiliated with Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed al-Nahyan and the IHC conglomerate, in a transaction valued at approximately £1.4 billion. Richard Caring remains as Executive Chairman and is publicly signalling aggressive international expansion (Annabel's New York is already confirmed).

This ownership transition is your single most important commercial window. New institutional owners almost invariably trigger supplier reviews, and DIAFA's capital base makes a multi-year programme of international rollouts now financially routine. A private-label rum pitched today will be evaluated by a group that has both the reason and the money to re-platform its beverage programme.

What we found that matters most

  • Ron Santiago de Cuba is the incumbent group-wide house rum across Scott's Mayfair, Scott's Richmond, Sexy Fish London, Bacchanalia, the Original Ivy, and The Ivy Asia. Dethroning RSdC at the "Carta Blanca / aged 11yo" positions is the core commercial contest.
  • Sexy Fish has own-label vodka, gin and tequila — but no rum. This is the single clearest white-space in the portfolio, and Sexy Fish is a format actively rolling out internationally under new ownership.
  • Scott's has an own-label Navy Strength gin. Caprice has already validated the private-label venue-brand thesis.
  • Clement Robert, Head of Beverage at The Birley Clubs, is a publicly-quoted advocate for "artisanal quality, transparent sustainable brand values and provenance." He is your single most important named contact.
  • Annabel's runs an annual September "For the Amazon" campaign with a rotating celebrity spirit partner (Patrón 2021, The Lost Explorer Mezcal 2022–23). There is a documented template for branded-partnership activation — no rum brand has ever filled this slot.
  • Caprice Holdings swung to a £5.9m pre-tax loss in FY2023 on record £97.1m revenue. Cost discipline is a live management priority. A house rum that holds price while widening gross margin is a direct answer.

Section 02

Strategic Context: Why This Is The Moment

A change of control always reshapes procurement. This one reshapes an entire industry.

The DIAFA acquisition

On 13 April 2026 — one week before this report was compiled — DIAFA, an investment vehicle of Abu Dhabi royal Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed al-Nahyan and connected to IHC, acquired a controlling majority of the entire Richard Caring UK hospitality empire. The headline consideration of £1.4 billion covers all three operating entities: Caprice Holdings Ltd, Troia (UK) Restaurants Ltd (The Ivy Collection), and 46 Berkeley Square Ltd (the Birley Clubs).

Richard Caring remains as Executive Chairman — a bridging appointment that suggests continuity of creative direction while capital structure, international expansion and supplier relationships are restructured under new institutional discipline.

Why it matters to a new rum brand

  • Supplier review cycles follow ownership changes as night follows day. DIAFA's team will examine the group's procurement stack. Incumbent spirits suppliers — many of whom hold listings dating back years under Caring — are newly exposed. A fresh proposition, priced competitively, with a private-label angle that widens gross margin, is exactly the kind of pitch a new CFO welcomes.
  • Global rollout creates volume. Caring has openly briefed on Annabel's New York, Scott's international sites, continued Sexy Fish expansion and an international push for Ivy Brasseries. DIAFA has publicly endorsed the strategy. A UK listing is now a platform for global listings — materially changing the size-of-prize calculation for any supplier.
  • The group is indebted. A £229m shared HSBC facility appeared in both Troia (UK) and Annabel's accounts in 2024. DIAFA's capital can refinance this; but until it does, management is under pressure to protect and expand operating margin. High-GP private-label spirits speak that language fluently.
Commercial urgency

A deliberately compressed decision window of four to eight weeks (late April through early June 2026) sits between the deal close and the first full supplier review. Early engagement and early sampling positions your brand as incumbent candidate rather than incoming challenger.

Wholesale economics of your £25 position

The margin argument

At £25 wholesale, served at 50ml in a £13 cocktail, your cost of spirit per serve is £1.79 (ex-VAT, 14 pours/bottle). The cocktail's total COGS including £1.50 of mixers is ~£3.29 vs a £13 selling price = 74.7% GP. On a £14 cocktail that's 76.5% GP.

On a straight 50ml serve at £13.50 (Scott's RSdC 11yo price point), the GP on your rum = 84%. That is 6–8 percentage points above the blended spirits GP typical at Caprice venues, and directly addresses the FY2023 cost-pressure narrative.

Cocktail trends that favour a new premium rum brand

  • Rum broke £1bn in UK on-trade value in 2024 — and dark rum is growing at +5% value YoY (CGA OPMS). The category has momentum.
  • Daiquiri renaissance at premium bars — treated as a "bartender's handshake" akin to the Martini in whisky bars. Split-base and clarified variations are the current experiment.
  • Rum is moving up-cocktail — Diplomático's February 2026 "After Dark" partnership with Le Magritte Bar at The Beaumont built four stirred, spirit-forward nightcap cocktails around aged rum. This is where Caprice rum cocktails are heading, and a premium aged rum positions perfectly.
  • Mojito is losing ground at the ultra-premium end. Labour-intensive, low-margin, tourist-skewed. Being replaced with rum Highball serves and rum Old Fashioned / Manhattan riffs — which work beautifully for aged brown rums.
  • Agave is eating share but has plateaued in the ultra-premium on-trade (Sexy Fish and Isabel Mayfair already do own-label tequila). Rum has runway that tequila has largely consumed.

Strengths & Weaknesses

A candid assessment of the group as a commercial target — what makes them attractive, and where the challenges lie for a challenger spirit brand.

Strengths

  • Scale & concentration. 55+ UK venues under one beneficial ownership — group-level listing agreements materially change a supplier's P&L.
  • Top-line momentum. Combined UK revenue has grown ~2× in five years (Troia especially); the group is a growth account, not a mature one.
  • Brand equity. The Ivy, Scott's, Sexy Fish and Annabel's are each category-defining brands in their segment — a listing here is a credential asset for any challenger spirit.
  • Private-label DNA. Already five own/co-brand spirits SKUs confirmed across the group (Sexy Fish vodka/gin/tequila, Scott's gin, Bacchanalia gin). Your thesis is not novel — it is proven.
  • Named receptive buyers. Clement Robert (Birley) has publicly endorsed the exact positioning your brand will take (provenance, craft, sustainability).
  • International expansion under new ownership. DIAFA has capital for Annabel's NY, Scott's international, Sexy Fish rollout, Ivy US. A UK listing becomes a platform for global orders.
  • Rum category momentum. UK rum broke £1bn in on-trade value (2024); dark rum up 5% YoY; Bumbu became top-10 rum brand at any tier.

Weaknesses

  • Entrenched incumbent rum (RSdC). Ron Santiago de Cuba is the deep house rum across every sub-brand with a full four-tier range. Displacement is non-trivial.
  • Caprice Holdings swung to a loss. FY2023: (£5.9m) PBT on record £97m revenue. Administrative costs grew £25m in one year. A new rum brand is asking the group to adopt an unknown product during margin pressure.
  • Management bandwidth constrained. Caprice filed accounts eight months late. The group is running a concurrent sale process, leadership restructuring, and international expansion. Buyer attention is a scarce resource.
  • Shared £229m debt facility. Material leverage. While DIAFA can refinance, legacy creditor relationships constrain procurement flexibility until that happens.
  • Long decision lead times at premium tiers. Private members' clubs (Birley) and flagship restaurants (Scott's, Bacchanalia) have glacial approval cycles. You may sell in one season and see first pour six months later.
  • Group already chose its category poster children. Tequila/agave and Japanese whisky were positioned as the "premium narrative spirits" at Sexy Fish; Bacchanalia did gin. Rum is the leftover category — this is simultaneously opportunity and category-second-class problem.
  • Limited public menu data for members' clubs and a few Caprice venues. Birley, J Sheekey, 34 Mayfair, Daphne's and Harry's Dolce Vita all run without published spirits lists. Sample-and-walk reconnaissance is required to close these gaps.

Opportunities

  • The Sexy Fish rum white-space. Most commercially clear entry — follow the gin/vodka/tequila template, extend to a fourth SKU.
  • The Annabel's Amazon September slot. A proven 4-week branded activation template with no rum brand ever in the slot.
  • Brasserie network conversion. 36 venues running the same Mojito = high-volume single SKU flip.
  • The DIAFA supplier review window. Late April–June 2026 is the highest-signal window of the decade for this group.
  • International expansion multiplier. Land a UK listing, ride the global rollout — Annabel's NY and Scott's international are imminent.
  • Cost-margin narrative. At £25 wholesale vs Caprice's implied £27+ for RSdC Carta Blanca, you are a cost-reduction pitch dressed as a brand upgrade.

Threats

  • Big-spirits countermove. Diageo (Zacapa), Pernod Ricard (Havana Club, Bumbu), Bacardi, LVMH (Eminente) and Brown-Forman (Diplomático) all have group-level relationships with Caring and will defend aggressively with promo support and marketing contribution.
  • Category growth decelerating. IWSR flagged that global spirits value fell 4% in 2025 — first year volume outpaced value. Premiumisation continues but slowing.
  • New ownership may consolidate vendors. DIAFA/IHC's house approach could be to reduce supplier count rather than add. Timing and narrative matter more than product quality.
  • Risk of "Bumbu effect". Bumbu's accessible-luxury success has arguably saturated a mid-premium zone — forcing true premium brands to work harder on differentiation.
  • Ivy Brasserie Mojito may not be network-durable. Menu rotation is seasonal; signature rum cocktails can be displaced at the next menu cycle without notice.
